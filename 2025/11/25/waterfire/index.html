By Vanson Vu | The Brown Daily Herald



A full WaterFire lighting on the Providence River in 2025 features fires blazing in 81 braziers — large, shallow metal stands just above the water’s surface. The braziers run from Crawford street to the WaterFire Basin in Waterplace Park, spanning around five blocks. Throughout the night, fire performers and gondoliers weave their way along the water, while visitors mill around at the river’s edge, stopping at food stalls, admiring locally crafted art or interacting with living statues.

But in December 1994, when Barnaby Evans ’75 organized the first WaterFire, the event looked very different.

When Evans attended Brown in the 1970s, the Providence River was buried under streets and buildings, Evans told The Herald. In the 1990s, the city invested significant resources into uncovering the river.

On Dec. 31, 1994, Evans created a sculptural art installation dubbed “First Fire,” placing and lighting 11 braziers along the river in order to attract people to the newly created waterfront and celebrate the 10th anniversary of a bygone Providence New Year’s Eve tradition called “First Night.” The event, funded out of his own pocket and held without prior approval from the city, ran until 1:30 a.m. in the new year, Evans recalled.

WaterFire chose to use pine wood for its spicy scent and distinct crackle. A full lighting can go through 15-20 cords of wood — over 30,000 pounds — in one night, according to Bob Hopkins, who has volunteered with WaterFire since 2008.


By Jake Parker | The Brown Daily Herald



In June 1996 came “Second Fire,” when the then-head of the city’s Parks Department offered Evans $990 to run the program during a week-long international art conference. “It became viral,” Evans said. “The community said, ‘Hey, we’d love you to do this on an ongoing basis.’”

From there, community members supported the creation of the WaterFire nonprofit in 1997 with Evans as the executive artistic director, the role he remains in to this day. Over 30 years later, that organization has 30 employees, 400 annual volunteers and a permanent arts center, along with an annual budget of $3.7 million.

The fires attract nearly 1 million visitors annually, creating $149 million in local economic output and $9 million in state tax revenue, according to the WaterFire website. For funding, the nonprofit relies on corporate sponsorships, community donations and, recently, city and state support.

But today, nearly three decades since its founding, WaterFire is struggling to stay afloat.

Last year, the Boston Globe reported that WaterFire was $750,000 over budget in 2023, putting the price tag of each event at $250,000. The fiscal year 2026 state budget includes a $400,000 line item grant to WaterFire.

Rising expenses have made lightings more costly, as the organization’s annual insurance prices rose to nearly $250,000 in 2024, according to the Globe. The organization also has to pay approximately $50,000 per year to the city of Providence for event security — previously written off by the citypolice details. Since 2023, WaterFire has cut 25% of its staff.

But the underlying issue seems to be, according to Evans, is a loss of revenue.

Mark Allan, the interim executive director of WaterFire Providence, told The Herald that he believes the decrease in funding is symptomatic of a yearslong economic trend of companies leaving Providence or scaling back support for WaterFire.

Out of the nine Rhode Island universities that have previously sponsored lightings, only the Rhode Island School of Design remains a sponsor, Evans said. Earlier this year, Brown stopped sponsoring a Family Weekend WaterFire amid budget shortfalls, The Herald previously reported.

WaterFire had around 75 sponsors this year, Kelly Pattison, the director of sponsorship and corporate relations, told The Herald. Contributions ranged from $250 for an ad to sponsorship of a full lighting. Sometimes sponsorship can entail lending chairs or vases — “It takes a village,” Pattison said.

She explained that planning for a lighting is a long, continuous process, noting that in late October, she was already lining up donors for next year’s lightings. The COVID-19 pandemic “rocked everyone,” she said. Corporations became much more cautious about how they spent their money, prioritizing seeing a return on their investment. As a result, sponsorship has shifted to being more community-based, she said.

Amid the se rising costs, falling revenue and dwindling sponsorships, WaterFire scheduled just six full lightings for this year, a marked decrease from nine full lightings in 2024, 2023 and 2022. This year’s schedule saw three 30-brazier Basin lightings, as well as two partial lightings, which only illuminate a smaller section of the river.

Some city leaders are trying to and state have stepped in andto help. The fiscal year 2026 state budget includes a $400,000 line item grant to WaterFire. Earlier this year, Mayor Smiley proposed the city purchase the WaterFire Arts Center building for $3.5 million and rent it back to the WaterFire nonprofit for $1 a year, provided the organization holds eight full and five partial lightings a year. On Nov. 4, the City Council Finance Committee approved the resolution, sending it to the full City Council for a vote on Nov. 20.


“The scale of the event gives the impression that it’s presented by the city or by the state,” Evans said. Visitors do not realize it is a nonprofit that handles everything from building the stages for performances to adjusting the height of the river for the boats, he explained.

The Herald spoke to staffers, vendors, performers and organizers to see what goes into making WaterFire possible.

At the basin:

By Jake Parker | The Brown Daily Herald



Liz Knights, fire performer

Fire performer Liz Knights has been lighting up the Providence River as part of WaterFire for over a decade. Knights’s company, Cirque de Light, now employs most of the fireball-twirling performers on WaterFire boats — and sometimes, Knights performs with them, she said.

Knights’s path to performance began while she was a student at Brown, where she joined the juggling club and found a community that encouraged her interest in circus arts and gymnastics. After leaving school due to illness, she began performing professionally and soon crossed paths with WaterFire’s original fire spinner, Spogga Hash. After asking him if she could join the act, she received a formal invitation from the organizers.

Since then, Cirque de Light has grown to include over 60 fire performers, with about 10 rotating through WaterFire performances each season. “They’ll light their fire prop off of one of the torches, and then perform in the basin, and then perform going up and down the river, which is really fun, because you get to see so many different people,” Knights said.

For Knights, the choreography and spectacle are part of what makes the event so rewarding — and surprisingly safe. “People think it’s way more dangerous than it is,” she said, adding that she’s gotten worse burns from household objects than the fire she performs with.

Her favorite part of the night is the sheer beauty of it all. “It’s incredibly immersive,” she said. “You're surrounded by the audience. You're surrounded by fire and music.”

Having performed at WaterFire for so many years, it feels “kind of like a family,” Knight said. She expressed gratitude that this year’s season was able to happen, but worried about the event’s uncertain future. “It’s one of those things where people might not recognize the effect until it’s gone,” she added.

“I just like making people happy,” Knights added. “I’m there to make them feel joy, awe, wonder. All those things are very gratifying.”

Barry Lima, Staff sergeant

This year’s Nov. 1 lighting featured a “Salute to Veterans” event at the basin. Barry Lima Jr., a veteran staff sergeant, was one of the torch holders at the ceremony. Around 50 veterans participated in this year’s event, lighting the braziers that line the river, according to Lima Jr.

Lima and his wife come down to WaterFire every year, and after seeing a call for veterans to perform at the Nov. 1 lighting, Lima signed up. The experience was incredibly meaningful, he told The Herald.

Lima recalled taking a photo with his late father at the same spot where he lit the torches on Nov. 1. “There’s a picture that I have — me and my dad before he passed away — and it was right where I was lighting the torches,” he said. “So this place is pretty special. You know, with my dad not being here, I think of him a lot when I come here,.” Lima said.

He added that he felt instantly connected to the veteran community during the ceremony. “Even just being down there — I didn’t know any of them — we all still felt like brothers and sisters. Strangers, but like we’ve all been through the same hardship of wearing the uniform and all the stuff that we go through.”

On Steeple St.:

By Mia Santomassimo | The Brown Daily Herald



Cathy Tague, owner of Pourever Creations

Cathy Tague is the owner of Pourever Creations, a small family business that works with acrylic paint and epoxy to create designs on everything from wine glasses to charcuterie boards. Using a technique called pour painting, Tague designs primarily ocean-themed glassware.

Pourever Creations started around seven years ago, when Tague’s husband suggested that Tague pour epoxy over acrylic paint to make a glassy finish. They began selling their creations at local crafts fairs and “Small Business Saturday,” an initiative by WaterFire to feature local businesses at their arts center.

Since being accepted as a WaterFire vendor, Pourever Creations has been at nearly every lighting.

“WaterFire is, by far, our biggest and best event that we do,” Tague said.

But the preparation process can be complicated, Tague said, given that often, vendors only find out if they’ve been selected for an upcoming lighting around a month in advance. Since she works with epoxy, which can take up to a few weeks to fully set, Tague emphasized that it’s “about a month-long process to really get things up and going.”

Tague added that while their ocean designs are most popular, she likes to design her products for WaterFire based on the upcoming event. For the Veterans WaterFire, she designed red, white and blue pieces for sale.

WaterFire has been crucial for building her business, Tague said, as she often gets repeat customers across multiple WaterFires. “Every year they come back and get a new gift for someone that matches what they brought them last year.”

She also emphasized “the excitement and energy” of event staff, attendees and other vendors. She recalled one rainy WaterFire night, when Waterfire staff and vendors tried to help each other keep their stalls dry.

“You’d think it would be the most miserable thing ever, but it’s one of our favorite memories,” Tague said. “You build a community.”

Tague expressed concern about the recent decrease in WaterFire lightings. “I’m a small business and I rely on WaterFire,” she said.

Selahattin Sep, Tigris Handmade

Selahattin Sep, founder and owner of shoe store Tigris Handmade, said that for him, shoemaking is “a family tradition.” His family has been making shoes for 700 years, he said, with “no glue, no synthetic materials, no machine (and) no electricity.”

“Everything is made by hand,” he said, explaining that different pieces of leather are hand-stitched together to construct the shoes.

This shoe-making method requires at least three days to produce five or six pairs. But the shoes he produces last a long time: “Six years old and they still look brand new,” Sep said.

Since the shoes don't use any chemicals, they can also be composted. The shoes won’t “end up in the landfill,” he said.

Since Tigris Handmade doesn’t mass produce its merchandise, Sep doesn’t have enough pieces to keep his shop open while he displays his work at WaterFire. Despite the effort, he said, “it’s fun to be here.”

“It's not all about making money,” he said, adding that often, it’s “not worth it” financially to be part of the event.

Instead, he continues to come back to WaterFire because of the diverse crowds it attracts, he said. He believes that the community he builds at these events is sometimes “better than money.”

On Canal Walk:

By Kaia Yalamanchili | The Brown Daily Herald



Andrew Anselmo, origami

Andrew Anselmo, a mechanical engineer and origami street performer, has been folding paper creations at WaterFire for over two decades. His journey with origami began “peripherally” as a child, but it wasn’t until a university conference in Turkey that he discovered the joy of folding for others after being asked by attendees to perform origami, he said.

“And next thing you know, I’m making a couple of bucks,” said Anselmo, who is now an origami master and street performer.

Anselmo first brought origami to WaterFire when he folded peace cranes along the canal after the September 11 attacks in 2001. “My primary and best gig ever has been WaterFire,” he said. Since then, he has returned year after year, driving down from Boston with his wooden box and knapsack, quietly folding for hours as crowds wander past, absorbing the music and firelight.

For Anselmo, the performance is about connection, not compensation. “You’re interacting with the crowd by giving stuff away,” he said. “I’ve got a sign on my little tip jar that says, ‘If you can’t leave a tip, leave a note,’ and that allows people to interact with you, and they don’t feel guilty if they don’t have any money.”

The reactions he receives are as varied as they are memorable. “I've had people say, can you make a ring for me, because I want to propose to my girlfriend,” Anselmo said. “I've had people come up to me saying, you know, this is my last WaterFire because I got cancer and I'm going to be gone.”

Despite the funding cuts, Anselmo remains committed to the event. “You need music. You need paints. You need sculpture … You need people doing all sorts of wacky stuff to make life worthwhile,” he said.

Juliana Craige, event day staff and cashier

Event-Day Staffer Juliana Craige has been working with WaterFire since June of 2023. She primarily works to help unload trucks and set up the WaterFire merchandise stand.

“The amount of work that it takes and coordination with the city to make a WaterFire happen is insane,” Craige said, pointing to staffers who come to set up early in the morning and the police that block off the streets the night before a lighting.

One of Craige’s favorite memories of setting up at WaterFire is of a lighting on a rainy day.

“We got halfway through setup, and it started torrentially pouring,” Craige said. From there, she and her bosses brainstormed a solution: to pull two trucks onto the street facing one another and set up the merchandise tents in the middle.

“We were working out of the trucks, and everybody was gathering under the tents,” Craige said. “It was super hectic, but super fun.”

On Washington St.:

By Jake Parker | The Brown Daily Herald



Tara Smith, living statue

Tara Smith, a performance artist and living statue, has spent the last 20 seasons bringing WaterFire to life — without moving an inch. She “fell into” the craft 21 years ago, she told The Herald, starting to busk in her hometown of Bridgetown, Massachusetts.

“I’ve always been a generally artsy person, but living statuary and performance art in general has been the art form that really gripped my soul,” Smith said.

Smith started out performing near the Washington Street Bridge, an area that wasn’t originally part of the event. But after a few years, WaterFire organizers reached out to her, inviting Smith to become a formal fixture of the event.

“It's central to my life at this point,” Smith said. “It’s been nice seeing the same people coming to WaterFire year after year. I have adults, they’re in their 20s now, talking about seeing me when they were a little kid.”

During performances, she scans the crowd looking for chances to connect — with a wave to a child or a gentle gesture to ease someone’s nerves. Her characters, Smith said, are pieces of herself she “pluck(s) out and put on display… and give them a moment to shine.”

WaterFire’s atmosphere sets it apart from other performance events, Smith said. “There is inherently this sort of unwinding at the end of the day with something fun,” encouraging reflection and small, meaningful interactions, she said.

For Smith, the WaterFire season provides the bulk of her annual income, and she relies on the event not just financially but creatively. Smith worries about the event’s future, particularly after funding cuts and the slowdown caused by the pandemic. “I rely on the WaterFire season to be the bulk of my income for the year,” she said.

But she remains committed. “I have no plans to retire anytime soon,” she said. “I’m going to keep performing until my body says, ‘No, we can’t do this anymore.’”

By Horatio Hamilton | The Brown Daily Herald



Matt Stone, Gather Glass

Since 2011, Matt Stone has been working with WaterFire as the co-owner of Gather Glass, a Federal Hill-based glassblowing studio that offers handcrafted products, lessons and more.

With help from event staff, Gather Glass originally sold “wearable necklaces and earrings,” Stone said, along with WaterFire-themed glasses. Over the years, Gather Glass’s live demonstrations and range of product offerings have evolved and grown.

WaterFire has “never been anything but wonderful,” Stone said. The festival brings together “such a big group of people from such a variety of places,” he added.

On the night of the Nov. 1 WaterFire, winds were gusty and strong, making setting up “a little tricky,” Stone said. He and his coworkers used “zip ties and sandbags” to anchor their tent and demonstration area in place.

Gather Glass has held live glassblowing demonstrations for its entire tenure at WaterFire. Equipped with a mobile furnace, two glassmakers often work on a piece at the same time next to the sales tent. That piece “could be a vase, or a cup or a bird,” said Stone.

“It’s a dance with the medium, and everyone can be part of something special” watching the demonstration, Stone added. “Sometimes we’ll have 50, 100 people watching us make a piece.”

WaterFire is “such an important part of our business,” Stone said. The festival provides not only a chance for attendees to purchase glass products, but also for people to “learn about who we are” and sign up for glassblowing classes, he added.

The financial boost that WaterFire provides Gather Glass “carries over” for “weeks and months beyond” the event,” Stone said. “The business that we get from WaterFire is huge.”

As a whole, WaterFire nights are “so important for the economy and the jobs that are here” in Providence, Stone said — not only the vendors, but also “the hotels and the surrounding businesses.” Despite funding changes throughout the years, Stone emphasized the consistency of “really big, fun crowds” that attend the festival.

“It brings this beautiful atmosphere and energy to the city that you just can’t replicate anywhere else,” Stone said. “When I think of Providence, I think of WaterFire.”

“Without it, our city would be a lot different,” he added.

Joseph Mushipi, visual artist

Hailing originally from Zambia, visual artist Joseph Mushipi has been participating in WaterFire since 2002.

“Nature is the source of my inspiration,” he said. “I paint magical trees. I paint leaves. I’m so obsessed with nature.”

Mushipi describes his experience with WaterFire as simply “phenomenal.”

“I sell my art to a lot of big festivals,” he said. But his experience with WaterFire in particular has helped him to “learn a lot and improve so much.”

Especially because of its frequency compared to other festivals across New England, the festival has provided him the most “exposure,” he said.

The process of preparing for WaterFire “takes so much work,” Mushipi explained. Throughout the year, he has to stay stocked with art materials and build a strong inventory of prints. The night of the festival, he comes early to set up his booth and string lights across the inside.

“It’s art on its own, just arranging the paintings on the wall,” he added. “So I love it.”

But his work has not come without financial difficulties.

“Especially this year, sales have gone way down,” he said. “Looking at what is happening in the country,” he believes that more people are choosing to spend their money on basic necessities such as food.

“But I signed up for this,” he added. “Two paintings, or prints, whatever comes out of it — you just say, ‘Thank God for that.’”

During WaterFire nights, Mushipi’s booth often grows extremely crowded. The event formally ends at 10:00 p.m., but Mushipi says that he usually stays until 11:00 p.m. to connect with lingering attendees.

“Watching people walk past, I see a lot of sad faces,” he said. “But the moment they come close to my art, their face changes. The customer came very sad, and he goes home very happy. I want to achieve that.”

Tommy Tainsh, Good Vibes

Tommy Tainsh, owner of beverage company Good Vibes, said that he and many of his coworkers “have worked for other companies that have done WaterFire … for probably 30 years.” He noted that the event has “really obviously grown” since he first worked there as a vendor.

In the late 1990s, Tainsh said, “they used to have a little dance floor” at WaterFire.

But Good Vibes is a new company, Tainsh noted. “This is our first summer, and it’s been great,” he said.

Their WaterFire menu advertised several seasonal offerings, including a hot apple cider with caramel whiskey and a spiced fall sangria. At the events they cater, Tainsh said, “we try to bring a little bit of everything.”

He said that Good Vibes tries “to do something a little extra special” for each event — for WaterFire that day, the company had “glow-in-the-dark ice cubes,” he said.

The company also sells several locally sourced beers, since for the last three decades, Tainsh has worked as a brewer. “We support a lot of local breweries, because it’s tough in this day and age,” he said.

Preparing for the event takes weeks, Tainsh noted. For WaterFire, Good Vibes made 50 gallons of hot apple cider.

In catering, Tainsh added, Good Vibes model its approach after the company name: they “bring the positive energy and hope for the best.”

“It seems like a five hour event, but it's more like a three week event,” he joked, adding that “it’s worth the effort, because when you have people and they enjoy what you bring ... that's all you could ask for,” he said.

WaterFire is “huge” for local businesses, Tainsh said. “Not everybody goes out anymore ... the new generation stays in,” he added, but WaterFire “is an excuse to come out.”

Tainsh said there may be a silver lining despite funding cuts: Rarer WaterFire lightings might attract more visitors. “If you have one every week, maybe as many people won't come,” he said.

At Good Vibes, Tainsh works with his wife Carlene, his sister Colleen and his friend Tony — who he described as “like a brother” to him.

“We’re not just here to make money,” he said. “We’re here as a family.”

At the WaterFire Circle Tent:

At WaterFire, “we don't have any fancy seats by the river where you get the best view. We don't sell seats on a boat to ride because it's meant to be seen by everybody from the shore,” Evans told The Herald.

WaterFire does, however, offer boat rides to members of the WaterFire Circle, their “most generous supporters.”

WaterFire Circle offers both individual and corporate membership plans. The lowest individual plan, which grants the purchaser 16 WaterFire Circle tickets, costs $1,500. The pricier gold plan costs $5,000, granting the purchaser 30 tickets and the right to reserve a private boat ride, according to their website.

Circle members also have access to the WaterFire Circle reception tent, a private seating area with refreshments, beverages and a private fire dancer show.

Jeanine Palumbo, volunteer with WaterFire Circle

Since 2007, Jeanine Palumbo has volunteered at WaterFire. She currently works at the welcome tent for the WaterFire Circle, which consists of individual and corporate sponsors that help to support WaterFire.

She said that the WaterFire Circle aims to make up for the recent decrease in donations for the event. While the state and city contribute funds, “it’s nowhere near what it takes to put on a WaterFire,” she said.

Palumbo said that people can become “members” of the WaterFire Circle, which allows them entry into the reception tent. “It’s the only place that you can have access to our wooden boats, where you can go in amongst the fire.”

Palumbo emphasized that while WaterFire is still struggling with their sponsorship, individual donations have become even more important. “It certainly isn’t going to affect the deficit we have in a big way, but it will all add up,” she added. She said that WaterFire does not charge an entry free, which allows the broader Providence community to participate in the event.

On Star Walk:

By Vanson Vu | The Brown Daily Herald



Sarah Voykovic, Star Field volunteer

Sarah Voykovic began volunteering with WaterFire in June. On the night of the Nov. 1 lighting, she was stationed at “Starry, Starry Night,” an art installation that suspends visitors’ wishes from light-up blue stars by Hemenway’s Restaurant to raise funds for WaterFire.

Voykovic explained that the Star Field offers several different types of products, including “little trinkets” with names like “dream orbs” and “dreamworms.” However, the main attraction is the dozens of stars hanging from the trees.

“People can take a ribbon, and then they write their wish on it,” Voykovic explained. “Then one of our team members will go and hang it in the Star Field, wherever the person chooses.”

With the many people that make Waterfire function, from volunteers to vendors and even attendees, the event intends to “use the arts to make the community stronger,” Evans said.

“I wanted to create an artwork that was about celebrating the values of community,” Evans added. “I wanted to make an artwork that didn't involve cynicism, that didn't involve irony, and it was absolutely welcoming to everybody, no matter what their level of understanding about the arts.”

Amid its financial challenges WaterFire is increasingly courting wealthy donors to keep the fires burning, according to Mark Allan, the interim executive director of WaterFire Providence. While Evans understands that this strategy is also employed by museums, operas, ballet companies and other arts organizations, he emphasized that “WaterFire is not a piece about the elite.”

“It’s about welcoming absolutely everyone of any means, from the homeless to the art professor to students, to (new) immigrants, to people who just wander in on the way home from the beach,” Evans said.

Having been at WaterFire for almost two decades, Palumbo said she has experienced many wonderful moments with the community, visitors and guests.

“There aren’t many situations where you can come out with a diverse group of people from all over the world to enjoy something in a peaceful, magical setting,” Palumbo said. “Between the music, the fires and the sounds, it ignites all of your senses simultaneously and shows Rhode Island in its best light.”

“The scale of the event gives the impression that it's presented by the city or by the state,” Evans said. Visitors do not realize it is a nonprofit that handles everything from building the stages for performances to adjusting the height of the river for the boats, he explained.

When asked by The Herald about his hopes for the future of WaterFire, Evans said that he has many ideas for expanding the event, such as creating a torch ceremony for Brown’s graduation.

“We have hundreds of ideas of things we’d like to try,” Evans said. “We’d love to bring back some of the new ideas we’ve never done, but we can’t do any of them without funding.”

For Evans, creating WaterFire was a “fascinating experiment,” going from lighting fires with borrowed boats to developing an entire crew of people to make the event function.

“I don’t think I ever imagined 31 years later we would still be doing it,” Evans said.
