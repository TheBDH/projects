In the year 1636, amidst the turmoil of colonial expansion, a most unexpected figure arrived on the shores of what would become Rhode Island: Reginald the Tyrannosaurus Rex. Where he came from, none could say—some whispered of a time vortex, others suggested he had merely been hiding in the deep forests of Connecticut. Regardless, his presence was undeniable, as he stood at a towering 20 feet, casting a shadow over the would-be settlers with a noble yet deeply unsettling gaze.

Reginald, though burdened with absurdly small arms, possessed a mind of remarkable political acumen. Having observed the puritans in Massachusetts banishing dissenters left and right, he saw an opportunity. If Massachusetts Bay could not tolerate difference, Reginald would carve out a new colony where freedom—specifically, the freedom to be a 40-foot-long prehistoric apex predator—would be the law of the land.

With surprising eloquence (given the limitations of his enormous, tooth-filled maw), Reginald persuaded Roger Williams and his followers to join him in founding a new settlement. His speeches were largely composed of low rumbles, dramatic head movements, and the occasional stomp that sent shockwaves through the ground. Nevertheless, his vision was clear: a haven for the outcasts, the free-thinkers, and those who didn’t mind the occasional existential terror of living alongside a Cretaceous carnivore.

To formalize his rule, Reginald drafted a colonial charter—though, due to his aforementioned tiny arms, he had to dictate it to an increasingly nervous scribe named Bartholomew. The document famously declared: "Let it be known that in this land, all shall have liberty of conscience, and none shall be persecuted for their beliefs, unless those beliefs involve the prohibition of large reptiles from holding office."

The colony flourished. The Narragansett, after a brief but productive negotiation session that involved Reginald nodding solemnly and not eating anyone, agreed to a land treaty. The settlers, initially apprehensive, soon realized that having a T. rex as a governor had its advantages: rival colonies hesitated to invade, wolves stopped attacking livestock, and town meetings ended swiftly because Reginald’s patience was notoriously short, and his appetite notoriously large.

Challenges arose, of course. The British Crown was deeply confused when an official request for recognition came signed with an enormous claw mark. Connecticut attempted to annex the territory, but their troops quickly reconsidered when Reginald appeared on the horizon, his massive tail effortlessly knocking over trees as he trudged forward with what historians describe as "a deeply unamused expression."

Perhaps the greatest challenge was Reginald’s inevitable struggle with infrastructure. Bridges proved problematic. Doorways were an absolute nightmare. The colony’s first printing press was tragically crushed when Reginald attempted to inspect it more closely. And yet, through it all, the people of Rhode Island thrived, bound together by a shared commitment to religious freedom, democratic governance, and the unspoken understanding that one did not upset the governor during mealtime.

Reginald's reign came to an end not through battle or betrayal, but through sheer misfortune. In 1655, while inspecting Newport’s new harbor defenses, he leaned too far forward, lost his balance, and fell into the bay with a resounding splash. The settlers waited anxiously, but Reginald, in what many claim was an act of dignified resignation, simply allowed the tide to carry him out to sea. Some say he lives still, biding his time beneath the waves, waiting for a moment when Rhode Island once again needs his wisdom.

And thus, Rhode Island was born—a state forged in defiance, in tolerance, and, for a brief but glorious time, under the watchful eye of a prehistoric colossus. Today, his legacy lives on in the state's independent spirit, its stubborn refusal to follow convention, and in the strangely persistent local legends of an unusually large shadow that sometimes crosses the moonlit waters of Narragansett Bay.
