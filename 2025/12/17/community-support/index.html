How the Providence community came together to support students in the wake of the mass shooting on campus

After the tragedy that unfolded Saturday evening, the Brown community showed up for one another by providing shelter, food, and financial and emotional support for students who needed it.

On Saturday, a tragedy shook Brown’s campus. Two students were killed and nine others injured in a mass shooting that occurred in Barus and Holley. In the aftermath, the Brown and Providence communities have stepped up for one another, from sheltering students to feeding them to raising money for victims.

Here’s how communities have joined forces to ensure the safety and wellbeing of everyone on College Hill since the incident.
Students constructed a memorial in front of the Van Wickle Gates on Sunday.

Providing shelter and comfort while sheltering in place

As news of the shooting spread across campus Saturday afternoon, several businesses in the area took students in, offering them shelter and food.

After hearing the sirens, eight students and five employees took shelter in the basement of Ceremony, according to Cheyenne Gansell, the sales and marketing specialist for the tea shop.

The staff dimmed the lights, pulled the curtains down and flipped the chairs onto the tables, sheltering in place for the next several hours. “We grew up knowing that that is what you do in America when guns or gun violence happens,” Gansell said. “You go and hide.”

Once the lockdown was lifted, Gansell and other employees drove students home, while some even housed students for the night.

“I think it was really important to be there in community, with the community, for the community,” Gansell said. She described how one of the students who had sheltered in the basement came by the store to buy one last matcha before she went home, thanking the employees for hiding her.

Coffee Exchange, a Providence coffee shop, took in about 30 people on Saturday night, most of them students, according to Owner Charlie Fishbein. He described how all the lights were turned off once the alert was sent out, and students were hiding in the bathroom and back stairwell.

“We were in the dark for seven hours,” Fishbein told The Herald, describing how everyone was quiet, passing the time on laptops and phones or reading books. “The atmosphere was surreal,” he added. Taste of India, a restaurant across the street, provided food later in the evening, which was served “buffet style with a flashlight.”

Students and staff began to leave the coffee shop at around 11 p.m., and Fishbein added he made sure everyone had a ride and a place to go.

Students who took shelter in the Olney-Margolies Athletic Center were met with support from several non-profit organizations, including the Red Cross.

Over the weekend, Red Cross volunteers provided food and refreshments for over 1,500 people, including “students, faculty and local responders,” according to a statement from the American Red Cross Connecticut and Rhode Island Region in an email to The Herald.

Julia Antony ’26, who was working at the OMAC when the shooting happened, noted that most students there “hadn’t eaten in hours” and “it was really comforting” when volunteers from non-profit groups showed up.

“The fact that they were there and they sacrificed their own safety to help us was really admirable,” Antony said.

‘Like my kids’: Housing students on the night of December 13

Many Brown staff and professors also opened their doors to students in need.

“Vio” Diniz, who has been a cashier with Brown Dining Services for over 20 years, was at home when she heard about the shooting through an email alert from the University. After learning that two students from a family she knew was on campus, Diniz said she invited them and other students to stay at her home until the shelter in place order was lifted.

“Brown students, they are like my kids,” she said.

Associate Professor of History Jeremy Mumford was in the middle of leading a HIST1976V: “History of Childhood” seminar of about 12 students at his home when the shooting occurred. Mumford and his wife, Professor of Biology, Data Science and Computer Science Sohini Ramachandran, told students they could stay the night at their house.

“My daughter, who’s 12, was very interested in making sure that everyone had a comfortable bed,” Mumford said, recalling how she “gave her bed” to students and “went around making up a bed on the couches.”

Because there wasn’t enough space in the couple’s house to shelter all the students, Ramachandran called a neighboring professor to take in half of the students. Friends of students in the class who “didn’t have anywhere to go” also arrived later at the house to take shelter, according to Mumford.

Sweeney, who graduated from Brown in May but lives in Rhode Island, offered her family’s home in Cranston to whoever needed a place to stay that night. Three students took up her offer. “The night was harrowing,” Sweeney said. She recalls sending hundreds of text messages checking in on her friends at Brown, and did not sleep.

The following night, when it was announced that the person of interest detained in the shooting was released, Sweeney sent out another wave of messages saying her home was open. A total of 10 people came over for dinner, while three ended up staying the night. Sweeney drove them to the airport the following morning. “I can’t be prouder to be part of the Brown community,” Sweeney said, expressing her appreciation for those who “couldn’t be here on the scene” but were still “giving rides, helping out, housing people.”

Others, like Adjunct Lecturer of Visual Arts Susanna Koetter, offered students seeking to return home a ride to the airport through Sidechat, an anonymous online forum for college students.

“I want to be a source of stability for my students (and) for all the students,” Koetter said.

‘It’s a small gesture’: Donating time and resources

The Undergraduate Council of Students is running a community assistance form which has received over 2000 responses as of Monday evening, according to UCS President Talib Reddick ’26. Most of the responses have been from alumni, parents and community members offering financial support, rides, housing and other supplies.

The idea for the form was proposed by First Year Representative Cindy Sun ’29, who has been “really leading the charge in terms of monitoring everything,” according to Reddick.

Sun said she first got the idea after her roommate’s mom offered her a ride and a place to stay. “I realized that I was really lucky to be part of a community,” Sun told The Herald. “I wanted to make sure that the people who didn’t have that, maybe didn’t really know the people in their dorm building or have someone they could reach out to for help, I wanted to make sure that they would be able to get a ride, a place to stay, food if they needed it.”

“People everywhere” — including Brown alum and community members at Columbia, Yale and Tufts — have offered support via the form, Sun said. Some mental health professionals have also reached out to offer counseling services via the form, she added.

Beyond the form, Reddick worked with Uber and Lyft to provide community members on campus with discount codes for travel.

“Our hearts are with the Brown University community,” said Senior Public Policy Manager at Lyft Brendan Joyce. “It’s a small gesture, but we hope a meaningful gesture to help folks with their transportation needs during this difficult time.”

‘Exemplifies the spirit of Brown’: Raising funds for victims and survivors

Samira Umurzokova, the sister of Mukhammad Umurzokov, one of the victims who was killed during the shooting, began a GoFundMe page which raised nearly $490,000 as of Wednesday to help with expenses the family will have to face. Any remaining donations will go towards a charity in his name.

Another fundraiser managed by Tiffany Netto MD’27 raised nearly $87,000 in a GoFundme to support the medical bills of hospitalized victims. “I am completely floored by how quickly money was raised,” Netto said.

The fundraiser is currently paused as Netto is working with President Christina Paxson P’19 P’MD’20 and deans at Warren Alpert Medical School to distribute the funds.

“I think this fundraiser really exemplifies the spirit of Brown and how, despite it being a big school in a big city, it really does have a small town community feel,” Netto said.

In the days following the shooting, students and alumni began efforts to raise funds for the affected victims and students trying to get home.

Autumn Wong ’25 — an alum living in Miami who started the Bruno Flight Fund GoFundMe — raised over $20,000 to assist with any additional travel costs. With these donations, Wong said she has been able to cover the cost of 22 flights as of Tuesday, and is working on getting in touch with Delta and American Airlines to rebook students’ flights.

Wong, who was a Meiklejohn and Community Coordinator as a student, began reaching out to her former residents and advisees who are still at Brown when she heard the news. She started covering flights for students out of pocket, and once her savings account was drained, decided to start a GoFundMe to recuperate the cost and cover more flights.

“I hope the students just feel really supported,” she told the Herald, adding that “it’s probably a great stress relief to (not) have to worry about one more thing and just focus on getting back to their families.”

Andrea Capotosto, senior director of finance, operations and analysis at the School of Public Health, was at home when she heard about the shooting.

Capotosto, who gave out “free mom hugs” outside the Sharpe Refectory on Sunday, was inspired by seeing “someone doing free dad hugs at a pride event a couple years ago,” she said. “I just saw the reaction of the people who were there, and I just found it so moving.”

“If my children were far away from me and they were scared, I would want someone else to do what I did,” she continued. “I wanted to remind people that for every tragedy that happens, there’s goodness out there, and people that want to help,” she added.

Daniel Soto Parra ’28, who started a petition to require ID swipes for all University buildings at all times, shared this sentiment.

“It should be how we come together as a community, rather than the violence, that defines this event,” he said. “There is no darkness that love cannot turn into light.”

‘Support and comfort’: Sharing meals and kind words

Kabob and Curry, a restaurant on Thayer Street, gave all students — and any community member who asked — a free meal last Sunday. Madhav Basnet, a chef and waiter at the establishment, said he was “very happy to serve” the Brown community and for the opportunity “to help.”

About 120 students came by for a free meal, according to Basnet. He checked to make sure everyone who ate at the store had a safe place to go after, and offered free food for them to take home. “Food matters,” he said.

Inside the Ratty, Brown community members came together to support victims of the shooting through a poster-making station that Soto Parra helped manage. Students wrote messages of support and encouragement for their peers recovering in the hospital on the posters.

In a message to The Herald, Soto Parra thanked everyone who contributed to the posters, adding that they were delivered to Rhode Island Hospital later that day.
Soto Parra helped manage a poster-making station to send messages of support and encouragement to students recovering in the hospital.
Joseph Oduro ’25.5, a teaching assistant for ECON0110: “Principles of Economics” who witnessed the shooting, expressed gratitude for peers who have provided “support and comfort” to him in the days following the tragedy.

“That has been extremely helpful, just knowing that I have a community behind me,” he said in an interview with The Herald. “And that’s the community that I love and will support and continue to try my best to protect at all costs.”

“Continue to lean on your brothers and sisters,” he said. “That’s going to be the best way moving forward, and feeling that support and feeling that love to really just be able to thrive on campus once again.”
