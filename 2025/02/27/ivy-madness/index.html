The Road to Ivy Madness

Can Brown's Basketball Teams Still Make the Tournament?

Reporting and web design by Caleb Ellenberg

On March 15th and 16th, Ivy Madness, the Ivy League championship basketball tournament, will come to the Pizzitola Sports Center here in Providence for the first time since the tournament was created in 2017. However, it remains to be seen whether either of Brown’s basketball teams will be playing on home court.

The Ivy League basketball season is entering its final stretch, and as of Friday, both Brown’s men’s and women’s basketball teams are on the outside looking in. The men sit in 6th place after their 3-game win streak was broken this Saturday night in a crushing loss to Cornell at the Pizzitola. The women’s team was also at home this weekend, and endured a similarly down to the wire defeat to Harvard. The women now sit in 5th place.

But neither team’s hopes are over. The men travel to Harvard and Dartmouth this weekend, playing two teams they hope to pass in order to qualify for Ivy Madness. The women are away at Columbia and Cornell. Although neither opponent is especially relevant to Brown’s position, those are also must-win games due to the dwindling opportunities to make up ground in the standings.

At the bottom of this website, The Herald data desk has built an interactive tool to explore the standings scenarios based on the outcomes of the final 12 games in each league, complex tiebreakers and all (those curious about the Ivy League tie breaking protocol can read more here).

The women’s team is looking to end an up-down season on an up and take that momentum into the tournament. Following Saturday’s loss, the women are on a 5-game losing streak and have only one more chance to avoid a winless month of February. Still, the women’s team showed early in the season that they are capable of playing at a high level. They went on a 3-game win streak in late January, defeating Cornell, Dartmouth, and Penn. The win against Penn was especially crucial, as Penn now holds the elusive 4th spot in Ivy Madness. Since Columbia, Harvard, and Princeton have already clinched the top 3 seeds in the tournament, Brown will have to pass Penn to get in.

Fortunately for Brown, there are a few ways to do this. Brown faces Columbia, who sit at the top of the standings, but also Cornell and Yale, who rest near the bottom. A 2-1 record looks reasonable, and a 3-0 record is possible, although it would require Brown to take down a 1-loss Columbia team.

Still, no matter how many games Brown wins, they will need help from Penn. The Penn women face Dartmouth, currently a 2-win team, but also Harvard and Princeton, who currently sit in second and third place in the Ivy with 9 wins.

If all games are won by the team with more wins, Brown will go 2-1, beating Cornell and Yale, while Penn will go 1-2, beating just Dartmouth. This would put Penn ahead of Brown by third tiebreaker, NCAA NET ranking, since the team’s would have an even head-to-head record and identical records against every other Ivy team (the second tiebreaker). However, NET rankings are updated by the NCAA after every game. Penn currently outranks Brown by just 13 places, 166 to 179, so Brown could conceivably gain the advantage on the NET ranking tiebreaker by season’s end.

Of course, all of this is moot if Brown defeats Columbia, or if Brown fails to do that and Penn defeats either Harvard or Princeton. You can explore all the other ways the standings might shake out with the interactive tool below!

Brown’s men’s team has had a similarly varied season. After a disappointing 2-5 start to Ivy play, Brown secured a signature double-digit win over Xaivian Lee and Princeton. Following the Princeton win, the team won 2 more games to bring their record to 5-5 and secure 4th place in the standings, owning the head-to-head tiebreaker over 5th place Cornell. Unfortunately for the Bears, their Saturday loss to Cornell not only dropped them out of Ivy Madness for the moment, but turned that critical tiebreaker into a draw.

Looking forward, the men’s team has a difficult but feasible path to the top four. Their next two games against Harvard and Dartmouth should be winnable, although Brown dropped their first game against each team, including a heartbreaking last-second loss to Dartmouth at home. Brown’s final home game of the season is against a formidable and currently undefeated Yale team. If Brown wins out, they will be in an excellent position to qualify for Ivy Madness. But given the difficulty of their schedule, Brown would be happy to go 2-1—then, they would need help.

If Brown beats Dartmouth and Harvard, they will have an even head-to-head tiebreaker with every team in contention for the final three spots in Ivy Madness. That will trigger the second tiebreaker, which is the best record against the highest ranked non-tied team. That would make a win over Yale incredibly valuable. Because there are many more teams in the race than on the women’s side, tiebreakers get more complex and many multi-team tie scenarios are possible—you can explore all those scenarios in the interactive tool below. The overall outlook is that Brown will probably need one of the bottom-feeders of Ivy basketball to knock off one of their competitors—Penn and Columbia each play both Cornell and Princeton, and if even one of those games is an upset Brown’s path to the playoffs becomes more promising.

For the Brown men’s team, the season is likely to come down to the final game against Yale. Win, and they're probably in. Lose, and the team is more likely than not left haunted by the Dartmouth loss and their collapse in the final minutes against Columbia. Will the team be left asking “what if?” or will they have a chance to redeem their painful loss to Yale in last year’s Ivy Madness? We can only wait and see.
